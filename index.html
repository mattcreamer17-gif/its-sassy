A read on where AI actually fits in Its Sassy's delivery stack. And where it doesn't.

Short version: build the folder spine first. Keep the creative layer human. That's the order most agencies get backwards.

The pattern we saw on your site.

You shipped fourteen blog posts in two weeks last December, then nothing for ten months, then fourteen more in two weeks this January. Noa is running Its Sassy, TLI Miami, and The Marketing Club at the same time. Roy is closing sales, running ops, and still writing content. The careers page is open for a graphic designer and a social manager. None of this is a brand problem. It's a capacity problem.

The pattern is familiar. We've seen it at 3-person shops, at 46-property operators, and at mid-market ops teams that grew past founder bandwidth.

Every campaign that ships well eats a week of founder time. The blog is the first thing to slip.

The 60 / 30 / 10 read.

Most agencies we've looked at are spending AI budget in the wrong place.

60%
Structured data work

Client briefs, influencer sheets, post-event reports, invoice prep. A folder, a template, a few rules. AI is great here.

30%
Judgment routing

Which influencer fits which brand. Paid vs. organic. Which client needs a call before they ask. AI is useful, not autonomous.

10%
Creative spark

The part only Noa and Roy can hold in their heads. AI is noise here. This is where agency output starts to feel generic.

Most agencies try to put AI on the creative layer first. That's why the output starts to feel generic. We'd flip it. Build the folder spine first, then let the creative layer stay human and distinctive.

Where we've done this before.

Last year we worked with a 46-property short-term rental operator with the same shape of problem: founder-led, ops-heavy, no engineering team. We scoped a tiered engagement built on folder-structured ops playbooks. The point wasn't to replace the founder. It was to get the founder out of the daily path on intake and reporting, so the book could actually grow.

Different vertical. Same mechanics as an agency running five to ten simultaneous campaigns.

The methodology.
